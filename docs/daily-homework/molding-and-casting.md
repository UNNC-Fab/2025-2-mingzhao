            <h2>1. Understanding Processes</h2>

            <h3>1.1 Existing Process: Injection Molding</h3>
            <p><strong>Injection Molding</strong> is the most standard manufacturing process for producing plastic parts in large volumes.</p>
            <ul>
                <li><strong>Principle</strong>: Thermoplastic pellets are melted and injected into a steel mold under high pressure. The material cools and solidifies into the final shape.</li>
                <li><strong>Key Features</strong>: High precision, extremely fast cycle times (seconds), and low cost per unit for mass production.</li>
                <li><strong>Limitations</strong>: High initial tooling cost (molds are expensive).</li>
            </ul>

            <h3>1.2 Latest Research: Freeform Injection Molding (FIM)</h3>
            <p><strong>Freeform Injection Molding (FIM)</strong> is a hybrid technology bridging 3D printing and injection molding.</p>
            <ul>
                <li><strong>Innovation</strong>: It uses <strong>3D printed soluble resin molds</strong>.</li>
                <li><strong>Process</strong>:
                    <ol>
                        <li>Print the mold shell using a specialized dissolvable resin.</li>
                        <li>Inject standard thermoplastic into the printed mold.</li>
                        <li>Dissolve the mold in a chemical bath, leaving only the complex part.</li>
                    </ol>
                </li>
                <li><strong>Advantages</strong>: Allows for <span style="color: #00f3ff;">complex geometries (undercuts)</span> that are impossible with traditional steel molds. Reduces prototyping lead time from weeks to hours.</li>
            </ul>
        </section>

            <h2>2. Materials Study</h2>

            <div class="material-box">
                <!-- Material A: Polypropylene (PP) -->
                <div class="material">
                    <h4>Material A: Polypropylene (PP)</h4>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Category</strong>: Thermoplastic.</li>
                        <li><strong>Processing Method</strong>: Injection Molding.</li>
                        <li><strong>Post-Processing</strong>: Gate trimming, flash removal. Surface finish depends on the mold texture.</li>
                        <li><strong>Effect</strong>: Semi-crystalline, translucent or colored. Extremely flexible, fatigue-resistant (living hinges), and chemically resistant.</li>
                        <li><strong>Application</strong>: Food containers, automotive bumpers, flip-top bottle caps.</li>
                    </ul>

                <div class="material">
                    <h4>Material B: Polyurethane Resin (PU Resin)</h4>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Category</strong>: Thermosetting Polymer.</li>
                        <li><strong>Processing Method</strong>: Silicone Molding / Vacuum Casting.</li>
                        <li><strong>Post-Processing</strong>: Sanding parting lines, painting, or plating.</li>
                        <li><strong>Effect</strong>: Can range from rigid to flexible (rubber-like). Captures extreme surface detail from the silicone mold.</li>
                        <li><strong>Application</strong>: Prototypes, action figures, small-batch electronic housings.</li>
                    </ul>

            <h2>3. Practical Operation Guide</h2>

            <div class="guide-section">
                <!-- Desktop Injection Molding Machine -->
                <div class="step">
                    <h4>3.1 Desktop Injection Molding Machine</h4>
                    <ol>
                        <li><strong>Setup</strong>: Turn on the heating element. Set the temperature based on material (e.g., PP at 230Â°C). Wait 15-20 mins for full melting.</li>
                        <li><strong>Clamping</strong>: Place the aluminum mold into the vise. <strong>Tighten securely</strong> to prevent flash (excess plastic leaking out).</li>
                        <li><strong>Injection</strong>: Pull the lever or activate the motor to force plastic into the mold. Hold pressure for 10 seconds to compensate for shrinkage.</li>
                        <li><strong>Cooling</strong>: Allow the mold to cool (air cooling). Unclamp and eject the part.</li>
                    </ol>
                </div>

                <!-- Silicone Molding & Casting -->
                <div class="step">
                    <h4>3.2 Silicone Molding & Casting</h4>
                    <ol>
                        <li><strong>Master Pattern</strong>: Secure your master model in a leak-proof mold box (made of LEGOs or acrylic).</li>
                        <li><strong>Mixing Silicone</strong>: Mix Silicone Part A and Part B by weight (e.g., 1:1 ratio). Stir thoroughly to avoid uncured spots.</li>
                        <li><strong>Degassing</strong>: Place the mixture in a <strong>Vacuum Chamber</strong> to remove air bubbles. This ensures a smooth mold surface.</li>
                        <li><strong>Pouring</strong>: Pour the silicone from a high point into the lowest corner of the box. Let it flow over the model naturally.</li>
                        <li><strong>Casting</strong>: Once the mold cures, remove the master. Mix your PU Resin and pour it into the silicone mold. Wait for it to cure and demold.</li>
                    </ol>
                </div>
